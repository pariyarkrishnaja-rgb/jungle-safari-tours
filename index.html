Nature Sanctuary

Chitwan National Park & History

Established in 1973, Chitwan National Park is Nepal’s first national park and a protected UNESCO World Heritage Site. Covering 952 square kilometers, it protects some of the last remaining populations of the Greater One-Horned Rhinoceros and the Royal Bengal Tiger.

How Life Used to Be (The 1981 Era)

When Krishna was born here in 1981, the relationship between humans and nature was entirely raw and beautiful:

Pure Nature

There were no concrete hotels. Villages were separated by 3 to 7 kilometers of dense forest.

The Grass Harvest

Once a year, the government opened the park for exactly one month. Villagers would enter to harvest tall elephant grass to rebuild their roofs and walls.

Living with Giants

Every single night, one member from each household had to sleep in a makeshift wooden tower (Machan) out in the farmlands. Their job was to protect the village crops from night-visiting rhinos, deer, and wild elephants.

Today's Ecosystem & Protection

Today, Chitwan faces modern challenges, but its magic remains. It acts as a critical sanctuary for biodiversity in South Asia, protected under the supervision of the Department of National Parks and Wildlife Conservation (DNPWC) and the National Trust for Nature Conservation (NTNC).

The park is a global refuge for the Greater One-Horned Rhinoceros, the Royal Bengal Tiger, and hundreds of bird and reptile species. Our native guide collectives work daily to clean the Rapti River, restore community forests, and run conservation awareness programs for local youth. For us, tourism cannot exist without protection.

Mammals: Strongholds of Giants

Chitwan is world-famous for protecting the Greater One-Horned Rhinoceros, boasting a thriving population of nearly 700 individuals. It is also one of the finest habitats on Earth for the endangered Royal Bengal Tiger.

Birds: Flyway Sanctuary

The park is a major global birdwatching hotspot. It supports over two-thirds of Nepal's globally threatened bird species, playing host to both resident birds and winter migratory species flying in from Siberia.

Reptiles: Living Relics

The Narayani and Rapti river systems flowing through the park support the Gharial—a critically endangered, thin-snouted, fish-eating crocodile—alongside the stouter Marsh Mugger crocodile.

Wildlife Guide

The Big Four of Chitwan: Tigers, Rhinos, Bears, and Elephants

Chitwan is one of the very few places on earth where you can walk on foot directly into the territory of Asian megafauna. Here is a guide on what to search for...

1. The Royal Bengal Tiger

Masters of camouflage. We track tigers by listening closely for warning alarm calls from spotted deer and langurs, and finding fresh pugmarks in the riverbank mud. They are solitary and respect distance.

2. The Greater One-Horned Rhino

Armor-plated giants. Though they look slow and heavy, they can run faster than a bicycle when charging. Always look for a stout, climbable tree when guiding near rhino zones!

3. The Sloth Bear

Easily distinguished by their long, white curved claws and shaggy black coats. They live primarily on termites and ants, but can be surprisingly aggressive if startled in tall grasses.

4. Wild Elephants

Majestic, nomadic bull elephants occasionally wander through the national park borders. They hold immense structural power and demand total distance and respect from trackers.

Travel Tips

When is the Best Time to Visit Chitwan National Park?

Planning your expedition to Nepal? Wildlife sighting depends heavily on seasonal shifts. Here is a breakdown of the best seasons to see the tigers...

🍂 October to March (The Best Weather)

Skies are crystal clear and the weather is pleasantly cool. Ideal for all safaris and general tourism in Nepal.

🌾 January to March (Best for Wildlife Spotting)

This is when local community farmers cut down the tall elephant grass to rebuild their clay houses. With the forest grass extremely low, visibility inside the jungle increases by 300%, making it the absolute prime window to spot tigers and bears.

☀️ April to June (The Hot Season)

Temperatures can cross 40°C, but because surface water dries out, large mammals are forced to crowd around the remaining deep water holes, offering intense, high-probability wildlife sightings.

The Story of Krishna Pariyar

Born in the Wild, Living for Conservation

Born in the Heart of Chitwan

My name is Krishna Pariyar, and my life began in 1981 in the untouched, pristine wilderness of Chitwan National Park. Back then, Chitwan was a completely different world. The forest was dense and untamed. Human settlements were sparse—just a few eco-friendly, traditional Tharu mud-and-thatch houses dotted the landscape, separated by vast distances of 80 to 150 meters. Neighboring villages were miles apart, connected only by footpaths and the sounds of nature.

Growing up, we lived in perfect harmony with the earth. There were no modern amenities; our lives depended 100% on what nature provided. We drank water directly from the flowing rivers, fished for our meals, and gathered wild vegetables and firewood from the jungle. Once a year, the government would open the national park for a single month, allowing villagers to harvest tall elephant grass to thatch our roofs and build our walls.

It was a time of true community. We had no money economy; we bartered our skills, food, and goods to survive. But living on the edge of the wild also meant sharing our space with giants. Every single night, one person from every household had to sleep out in the fields, just two steps away from the dense forest, to guard our crops from wandering rhinos, deer, and wild elephants. It was a beautiful, raw, and naturally balanced existence—humans ruled the day, and the wildlife reclaimed the villages at night.

The Early Sparks of Tourism

As a young boy, I witnessed the very birth of tourism in Chitwan. I vividly remember the first adventurous travelers arriving in big, rugged overland safari vans, carrying everything they needed. They would swim in the Rapti River and camp under the stars on the sandy banks. I used to play with these travelers, while my father worked tirelessly as one of the early pioneers, guiding them safely into the deep jungle. Watching my father share the magic of our home with the world sparked a deep passion within me.

A Life Dedicated to Conservation

In March 2007, I officially followed in my father’s footsteps and established Jungle Safari Tours. My vision was simple: to provide authentic, raw, and ethical wilderness experiences without any corporate middlemen.

Over the decades, my bond with the forest only grew deeper. I transitioned from being just a guide to becoming an active conservationist. I have proudly participated in:

  • National Tiger Census Operations: Tracking and counting the majestic Royal Bengal Tigers.
  • Churia Hills Wildlife Surveys: Mapping out remote ecosystems.
  • Avian Research: Conducting critical bird surveys to monitor biodiversity.
  • Guide Training & Education: Sharing my decades of jungle knowledge to train the next generation of local guides and conservationists.

Every year, when the monsoon rains flood the region, our team leads river and community forest cleaning campaigns, alongside wildlife awareness programs for the local youth. For me, tourism cannot exist without protection. That is why 10% of all our profits go directly back into local skill enhancement programs, helping our community grow together.
